Executive Summary

Six offerings ahead of the federal standard

On February 13, 2026, the U.S. Department of Labor released its AI Literacy Framework (TEN 07-25), establishing a national standard for AI literacy education across the American workforce and education systems.

The framework defines AI literacy as "a foundational set of competencies that enable individuals to use and evaluate AI technologies responsibly, with a primary focus on generative AI, which is increasingly central to the modern workplace."

DESN 374: AI + Design, offered through Eastern Washington University's Visual Communication Design program, has been taught since Fall 2023 and is currently in its 6th offering (Winter 2026), with its 7th offering planned for Spring Quarter 2026. This report demonstrates that DESN 374 already meets every foundational content area and every delivery principle outlined in the federal framework — and in several areas, exceeds it.

Foundational Content Areas

How DESN 374 addresses each of the five areas the DOL identifies as essential AI literacy content.

01 Understand AI Principles Complete
DOL: "A foundational component of AI literacy is developing a clear grasp of what artificial intelligence is and how it works… the vocabulary and mental models needed to understand how today's AI tools operate."
DOL Sub-AreaDESN 374 Course Element
Pattern recognition & probabilistic outputs ✓ Phase 1 "Immerse" (Weeks 1-5): Students test 6 different AI models, directly observing how the same prompt produces different outputs across systems
Capabilities and modalities ✓ AI Field Guide project: Students document text, image, video, and code generation capabilities across GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.5, Gemini 3.0 Pro, and 3 additional models
Training and inference ✓ Week 1 "Surveying the Landscape": Students research and map the AI ecosystem, understanding how models are built and deployed
Hallucinations and accuracy limits ✓ Week 2 "Calibrating Trust": An entire week dedicated to model testing and understanding reliability limits
Human design and oversight ✓ 4D Framework "Delegation" pillar: Students learn to decide whether, when, and how to engage with AI — centering human judgment
Strength of Alignment: Complete — Phase 1 is structured entirely around building mental models of how AI works through direct experimentation with multiple systems.
02 Explore AI Uses Exceeds
DOL: "A core element of AI literacy is understanding how AI is being used across real-world workplace settings."
DOL Sub-AreaDESN 374 Course Element
Productivity tools ✓ Students use AI for drafting, research synthesis (NotebookLM), and documentation throughout all phases
Information support ✓ NotebookLM for research synthesis; LLMs for background research and source management
Creative assistance ✓ Core to the course — Midjourney, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, Firefly for image generation; Runway, Kling, Veo, Sora 2 for video
Task-specific applications ✓ Vibe coding (v0, Bolt, Lovable), code assistants (GitHub Copilot, Cursor), no-code app builders (Figma Make)
Decision-support systems ✓ Students use AI for project planning, research direction, and design decision-making throughout Phase 3
Strength of Alignment: Complete and Exceeds — DESN 374 goes beyond workplace productivity to encompass creative, technical, and multi-modal AI applications across an exceptionally broad tool ecosystem.
03 Direct AI Effectively Complete
DOL: "A core element of AI literacy is understanding how to interact with AI systems in ways that produce useful and relevant results."
DOL Sub-AreaDESN 374 Course Element
Contextual framing ✓ 4D Framework "Description" pillar: Effectively describing goals to prompt useful AI behaviors and outputs
Prompting techniques ✓ Week 3 "Directing Dialogue": Dedicated week on prompt craft and voice development. Prompt Engineering is a named PLO-2 competency
Supplying relevant input ✓ Students learn to provide design briefs, style references, and contextual parameters to AI systems
Iterating on outputs ✓ Built into every project — the studio format requires iterative refinement of AI-generated work
Avoiding vague prompts ✓ Grading policy explicitly states: "Prompt quality matters — minimal effort results in weak outcomes and lower grades"
Strength of Alignment: Complete — Prompt engineering is not just covered but is a named technical competency with an entire week of focused instruction and ongoing practice.
04 Evaluate AI Outputs Complete
DOL: "An essential part of AI literacy is learning how to assess the quality and usefulness of AI-generated outputs."
DOL Sub-AreaDESN 374 Course Element
Verifying factual accuracy ✓ AI Use Expectation #2: "All AI-provided information must be validated with credible sources"
Assessing completeness and clarity ✓ Self-evaluation "Authorship & Synthesis" category (35%): Students assess intentionality, iteration, and coherence of AI-augmented work
Spotting gaps or logical errors ✓ 4D Framework "Discernment" pillar: Accurately assessing the usefulness of AI outputs and behaviors
Aligning with strategic intent ✓ Week 2 "Calibrating Trust": Students develop frameworks for when to trust and when to override AI outputs
Applying human judgment ✓ Course philosophy: "AI as a design partner, not a replacement for human creativity" — human judgment is positioned as essential throughout
Strength of Alignment: Complete — The self-evaluation system (40% of the total grade) creates a sustained practice of critical output assessment that exceeds typical training approaches.
05 Use AI Responsibly Complete
DOL: "Responsible use of AI is a core component of AI literacy… recognizing the limits of AI authority, protecting sensitive data, complying with workplace or legal requirements, and maintaining accountability for outcomes."
DOL Sub-AreaDESN 374 Course Element
Protecting sensitive information ✓ Academic integrity policies address appropriate use boundaries and data handling
Following workplace policies ✓ Mandatory compliance with EWU academic integrity standards; work must be produced within course dates
Avoiding misuse or harm ✓ 4D Framework "Diligence" pillar: Taking responsibility for what we do with AI and how we do it
Managing context-specific risks ✓ Students develop "ethical frameworks for AI use in design contexts" (named learning outcome)

Strength of Alignment: Complete — The mandatory disclosure requirement on every assignment creates a habit of accountability that directly mirrors professional responsible AI use.

Critical Context

Bridging the How Gap

"The DOL framework tells you what workers should be able to do… But it doesn't explain how they develop that capability."

Education policy analyst Tina Austin, who co-authored the California Department of Education's AI guidance, identifies a fundamental gap in the DOL framework. It operates as a top-down model — defining competencies from policy goals downward — valuable for setting national standards, but silent on how learners actually build these skills across different contexts, expertise levels, and starting points.

Austin also frames a broader transatlantic divide: U.S. frameworks prioritize empowerment and adoption, while EU approaches emphasize protection and governance. The DOL framework sits squarely in the empowerment camp — but empowerment without pedagogy is aspiration without a path.

DESN 374 answers this critique directly. The course provides the "how" through studio-based experiential pedagogy, progressive autonomy that moves learners from guided instruction to full independence, and the 4D Framework as a pedagogical scaffold. Where the DOL defines the destination, DESN 374 builds the road.

Beyond the Baseline

Where DESN 374 Exceeds the Framework

The DOL framework is designed as a baseline for broad workforce AI literacy. DESN 374, as a university-level course in a professional design program, goes beyond this baseline in several significant ways.

Exceeds 01

Self-Evaluation System

40% of the grade is self-evaluation. Students progressively take ownership of their own assessment criteria — by the final evaluation, they design their own rubrics aligned with personal goals. The DOL framework does not address assessment methodology at this depth.

Exceeds 02

Meta-Creation

In the Envision phase, students design AI-enhanced assignment briefs for others. This "teaching the teacher" approach represents a higher-order application of AI literacy — not just using AI, but designing AI-integrated learning experiences.

Exceeds 03

Multi-Modal Creative Production

While the DOL framework focuses on workplace productivity, DESN 374 encompasses image generation, video production, web development, interactive design, and cross-platform creative systems — a significantly broader and more ambitious scope.

Exceeds 04

Research Paper Requirement

The Deep Dive research paper (3,000–5,000 words, 10+ sources) adds scholarly rigor not contemplated by the DOL's workforce-training orientation. Students connect creative practice to design theory and critical analysis.

Exceeds 05

Portfolio-Ready Artifacts

While the DOL framework aims to produce AI-literate workers, DESN 374 produces portfolio-ready creative professionals with documented AI collaboration processes. Every project is designed to be professionally presentable.

Exceeds 06

Progressive Autonomy Model

The deliberate shift from instructor-led to student-led learning is a sophisticated pedagogical approach that exceeds the DOL's delivery principles — a transfer of agency that prepares students for self-directed professional growth.

Multi-Framework Alignment

Beyond Federal: California's AI in Education Framework

DESN 374 also aligns with the most comprehensive state-level AI literacy framework in the nation.

The California Department of Education's AI guidance represents what Austin characterizes as a "bottom-up" approach to AI literacy — developmental, learner-centered, and focused on building capability through progression. Where the DOL framework defines workforce competencies, California's framework addresses how learning should be structured. DESN 374 satisfies both.

CDE Pillar

AI Literacy for All (Five Big Ideas)

Phase 1 covers all five AI4K12 Big Ideas: Perception (image generation), Representation & Reasoning (LLM exploration), Learning (model comparison across 6 systems), Natural Interaction (prompt craft and voice development), and Societal Impact (ethical frameworks).

CDE Pillar

Academic Integrity Standards

Mandatory AI disclosure on every assignment — tools used, sample prompts, and how AI contributed. Defined acceptable and unacceptable use boundaries. Missing disclosure may constitute an academic honesty violation.

CDE Pillar

Equity & Access

In-person lab environment (Catalyst Room 209) provides device and broadband access, eliminating the digital divide. Progressive autonomy model accommodates varied starting points — no student is left behind in the transition to AI fluency.

CDE Pillar

Human-Centered Approach

Course philosophy: "AI as a design partner, not a replacement for human creativity." The 4D Framework centers human judgment in every pillar — Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence all begin with human decision-making.

CDE Pillar

Professional Learning

Six iterative offerings since Fall 2023 demonstrate continuous professional development in action. The instructor models the same continuous learning posture expected of students, using "do it with me" pedagogy rather than lecture-based delivery.

DESN 374 satisfies both the DOL's top-down competency model and California's bottom-up developmental approach simultaneously — evidence that the course's pedagogical design is robust enough to meet multiple independent frameworks.

Conclusion

A model implementation, six offerings and counting

DESN 374: AI + Design is not merely aligned with the U.S. Department of Labor's AI Literacy Framework — it is a model implementation that anticipated federal priorities across six offerings since Fall 2023. The course addresses every foundational content area, implements every delivery principle, and extends beyond the framework in areas of assessment, meta-creation, multi-modal production, scholarly rigor, professional preparation, and pedagogical sophistication.

As the DOL notes in TEN 07-25, the framework is designed to "allow for flexibility and adaptation across industries, roles, and contexts." DESN 374 demonstrates what that adaptation looks like in a creative professional education context — satisfying not only the federal framework but also California's state-level AI guidance, and answering the critical "how" question that policy analysts have identified as the framework's key gap.

The convergence of multiple independent frameworks around the same pedagogical practices is not coincidental — it is evidence that DESN 374 is built on sound principles. The course offers a compelling case study for how higher education can lead in building AI-literate graduates ready for an AI-driven economy.
